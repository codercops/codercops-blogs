## Introduction

The FDA has approved Neuralink to conduct a first-in-human clinical trial of its technology in people with quadriplegia. The trial will involve implanting Neuralink's N1 Link device in the brains of up to 10 people. The device is designed to record neural activity and transmit it wirelessly to a computer. This could allow people with quadriplegia to control computers and mobile devices with their thoughts. The trial will also involve testing the safety of the device and its ability to record neural activity. The trial will be conducted at the University of California, San Francisco (UCSF) and is expected to begin in 2020.

## The Transformative Potential of Brain-Computer Interfaces

Brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) have the capacity to redefine the way we engage with our surroundings. By enabling individuals with paralysis to control computers and mobile devices using their thoughts, BCIs hold the promise of granting newfound independence and communication possibilities. Furthermore, BCIs can be employed in the treatment of various neurological disorders, presenting opportunities for improved quality of life.

For instance, BCIs could help individuals with Parkinson's disease manage their tremors or assist those with spinal cord injuries in regaining some degree of mobility. Additionally, BCIs may have therapeutic applications for mental health conditions such as depression and anxiety.

## Mitigating the Risks of Brain-Computer Interfaces

While the potential benefits of BCIs are substantial, it is crucial to acknowledge the associated risks. One significant concern is the possibility of BCIs being susceptible to hacking, which could compromise an individual's thoughts or actions. Additionally, the risk of brain damage must be addressed and mitigated to ensure the safety and well-being of BCI users.
